Advanced Politeness, Impoliteness, and Mock Impoliteness Quiz

Question 1 of 10

1. Brown & Levinson’s politeness theory defines "negative face" as freedom of action. How does this contrast with the critique that in Chinese culture, acts like offering or inviting are not face-threatening?

2. In a meeting, a colleague says, "I hate to interrupt, but could we move this along a bit faster?" Which negative politeness strategy is this, and how does it mitigate a face threat?

3. A teacher tells a student, "Your essay was a complete disaster." Is this positive or negative impoliteness, and how does it align with Culpeper’s definition of impoliteness?

4. During a family dinner, Uncle Joe says to his nephew, "Still can’t cook, huh? This tastes like cardboard!" The nephew grins and says, "At least it’s not your cooking!" How does this illustrate mock impoliteness?

5. A stranger on a bus says, "Excuse me, sir, would you mind lowering your voice just a tad?" Which strategy best reflects this as politeness in a socially distant context?

6. Consider this exchange:
- A: "Let’s stop for a coffee, yeah?"
- B: "What, you think I’m your servant or something?"
How does B’s reply transform A’s politeness into impoliteness, and which face is targeted?

7. One critique of Brown & Levinson’s theory is its reliance on invented examples. Why does this limit its validity, and how might analyzing real conversations improve it?

8. A roommate says, "Wow, it’s amazing how dishes just pile up around here." How could this off-record statement become impolite, and what factors determine its impoliteness?

9. At a job interview, the interviewer says, "Good effort, champ, but we need more experience." Why might this be impolite despite its positive tone, based on mismatched social expectations?

10. Indirectness varies culturally. Create a scenario where a direct request is polite in one culture but impolite in another, and explain using politeness concepts.
